\section{Introduction}

In computer vision and computer graphics, artistic image style transfer has long been a compelling research direction. Given a content image and a style image, the goal of style transfer is to synthesize an output image that preserves the semantic structure and spatial layout of the original scene while faithfully embodying the visual style of the reference image---including texture, brushstrokes, and color palette.

The development of style transfer has undergone a paradigm evolution from non-parametric texture synthesis [1], optimization-based neural methods [2], and feedforward convolutional networks [3,4] to generative adversarial networks (GANs) [5,6,7]. Each paradigm has brought notable improvements in generation speed and transfer quality, yet inherent limitations remain: GAN-based methods are typically confined to style domains seen during training and struggle to generalize to arbitrary styles, while optimization-based methods incur heavy computational costs unsuitable for real-time applications.

In recent years, the rise of diffusion models [8,9,10] has greatly propelled progress in generative modeling. Stable Diffusion [11] and its successors leverage the latent diffusion mechanism and cross-attention modules to generate high-resolution, semantically rich images from text prompts. Inspired by this, researchers have extended diffusion models to style transfer tasks [12,13,14,15,16,17]. However, existing diffusion-based methods still face several notable challenges. First, methods such as InST [12] and StyleDiffusion [13] require gradient-based fine-tuning or textual inversion for each new style image, with per-image style adaptation often being time-consuming---limiting their applicability in large-scale or real-time scenarios. Second, some methods struggle to reliably preserve the spatial layout and semantic structure of the original image when applied to latent diffusion models, potentially altering the geometric form of the content while transferring texture. Third, although self-attention operations can effectively transfer local texture patterns, they often ignore global color statistics, leading to outputs that lack overall tonal coherence. Furthermore, most existing methods lack a mechanism for flexibly adjusting the trade-off between style intensity and content fidelity, limiting users' creative freedom.

To address these challenges, we propose AdaIN StyleKV---a training-free style transfer framework built upon pretrained diffusion models that requires no additional fine-tuning or textual inversion, and achieves favorable stylization results under our experimental settings. This research initially began with a relatively straightforward naive AdaIN prototype: under SDXL and Canny ControlNet structural constraints, combined with InstantStyle's effective block selection insight, AdaIN was applied to modulate text and image conditional features at target UNet blocks, with the number of target blocks configurable as 1 or 2. This prototype improved color coordination but still fell short in transferring local texture and brushstroke patterns. Building on this observation, we further introduce a selective KV fusion mechanism inspired by Style Injection [16], forming the final AdaIN StyleKV framework. For structure preservation, the framework incorporates Canny edge detection-guided ControlNet, which effectively enhances semantic content stability by preserving the edge and contour structures of the content image during generation. For texture transfer, all StyleKV experiments prefixed with \texttt{proc} in this paper are fixed at the target block \texttt{up\_blocks.0.attentions.1} for KV fusion, only varying the number of self-attention processors participating in fusion within that block; we cache the key and value features of the style image in these processors and perform weighted fusion with the corresponding features of the content image, thereby enabling effective transfer of local texture patterns. For color consistency, we take AdaIN as the methodological starting point and extend it into a cross-modal feature fusion module between text and image conditions, performing adaptive normalization fusion on the already-embedded text and image features to enhance color style transfer and overall atmospheric harmony. Furthermore, by introducing a fusion coefficient $\gamma$, the framework provides users with intuitive and continuous control over style intensity, enabling flexible trade-offs between stylization degree and content preservation.

We conduct component ablation, variant comparison, and parameter sensitivity analysis of our method on the MS COCO [18] content dataset and WikiArt [19] style dataset. Quantitative evaluation covers multiple dimensions including Art-FID [20], FID [21], and LPIPS [22]. Experimental results demonstrate that AdaIN StyleKV achieves a favorable balance between style fidelity and content preservation; notably, the StyleKV configuration with 5 self-attention processors in \texttt{up\_blocks.0.attentions.1} achieves the lowest Art-FID (85.43) without AdaIN, while the full method reduces LPIPS to 0.599 after incorporating AdaIN, indicating milder perceptual change. Additionally, through multiple comparative experiments, we systematically reveal how different configurations (e.g., number of target blocks, number of KV fusion processors within \texttt{up\_blocks.0.attentions.1}, fusion coefficient $\gamma$, presence or absence of the AdaIN module) affect style transfer outcomes. To enhance the method's practicality and interactivity, we also implement an interactive Gradio-based frontend demonstration system that supports real-time adjustment of style intensity, ControlNet parameters, and AdaIN fusion coefficients.

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Neural Style Transfer}

Neural style transfer was pioneered by Gatys et al. [2], who used Gram matrix matching of VGG [23] features through iterative optimization for style transfer. Subsequent work mainly focused on improving speed and generalization: Johnson et al. [24] introduced feedforward image transformation networks based on perceptual losses, achieving real-time style transfer; Huang and Belongie [4] proposed adaptive instance normalization (AdaIN), enabling feedforward arbitrary style transfer. Other notable approaches include attention-based methods [25], Transformer-based methods [26], reversible flow-based methods [27], and dual style learning frameworks [28]. Although these methods significantly improved speed over the optimization-based approach of Gatys et al., they still struggle with highly abstract or non-photorealistic styles.

\subsection{GAN-Based Style Transfer}

Generative adversarial networks have achieved remarkable progress in unpaired image-to-image translation. CycleGAN [5] introduced cycle-consistency loss, enabling cross-domain image translation without paired data; StarGAN [6] further extended this to a multi-domain translation framework capable of handling translation among multiple target domains with a single model. In addition, high-quality generative models represented by StyleGAN [7] and its variants [29,30] provide fine-grained and interpretable latent space control, widely used for in-domain image editing and attribute manipulation. It should be noted, however, that StyleGAN is fundamentally an unconditional generative model whose design objective is not cross-domain translation; when applied to out-of-domain style transfer, it often requires additional fine-tuning or feature disentanglement strategies, making it difficult to directly achieve arbitrary style generalization comparable to translation methods such as CycleGAN.

\subsection{Diffusion-Based Style Transfer}

Diffusion models have become the dominant paradigm for high-quality image generation. Among them, latent diffusion models (LDM) [11] achieve efficient training and inference by compressing images into a low-dimensional latent space. For style transfer tasks, researchers have proposed various diffusion-based methods:

InST [12] employs textual inversion to embed style images into the text vector space, effectively preserving style features, but requires per-style optimization. StyleDiffusion [13] introduces CLIP-based loss functions and fine-tuning strategies to disentangle content and style. DiffStyle [14] is a training-free method operating in the H-space of diffusion models; however, applying it to Stable Diffusion may alter the semantic layout of images. Style Injection [16] achieves training-free style transfer by replacing key--value features in self-attention layers with style image features, preserving content structure to some extent but lacking explicit structural constraints akin to ControlNet and having limited color transfer capability. InstantStyle [17] discovers that only specific UNet blocks play a critical role in style transfer and achieves efficient style injection through feature embedding at these blocks. StyleStudio [31] further explores selective control of style elements in text-driven style transfer.

\subsection{Conditional Control in Diffusion Models}

ControlNet [15] proposes a neural architecture that adds spatial conditional control (edges, depth, pose, etc.) to diffusion models using zero-convolution layers. It locks the original model and creates a trainable copy, ensuring that training does not disrupt pretrained features. We integrate the Canny edge variant of ControlNet to preserve the structural contours of the content image while performing style transfer. It is important to note that the ControlNet used must match the base model version. All experiments in this paper are based on the Stable Diffusion XL (SDXL) architecture, using its corresponding Canny ControlNet weights \texttt{diffusers/controlnet-canny-sdxl-1.0}; for the SD1.5 architecture, matching weights such as \texttt{lllyasviel/control\_v11p\_sd15\_canny} should be used instead. SDXL is chosen as the base model to leverage its higher generation resolution and stronger semantic expressiveness.

\section{Method}

This section details our proposed AdaIN StyleKV method. We first introduce the overall framework, then successively present the core components: selective KV fusion, cross-modal AdaIN, and ControlNet integration.

\subsection{Overall Framework}

Our method is based on Stable Diffusion + ControlNet (Canny), combined with cross-modal AdaIN and selective KV fusion mechanisms. The overall framework is divided into the following five stages:

\medskip
\noindent{\bfseries Stage 1: Image Preprocessing and Encoding.} Given a content image and a style image, we first extract the Canny edge map of the content image as the spatial condition input to ControlNet. Then, the VAE encoder encodes both the content and style images into the latent space.

\medskip
\noindent{\bfseries Stage 2: DDIM Inversion and Latent Trajectory Construction.} We use the DDIM sampler to invert the content and style images separately, obtaining their latent trajectories at each timestep, which provide input for subsequent cross-modal AdaIN fusion and progressive KV feature caching.

\medskip
\noindent{\bfseries Stage 3: Cross-Modal AdaIN of Text and Style Conditions.} During conditional injection at each DDIM inversion timestep, rather than adopting an adapter-based approach, we apply adaptive instance normalization (AdaIN) at the target UNet blocks identified by InstantStyle to perform cross-modal fusion of the already-embedded text features and image features.

\medskip
\noindent{\bfseries Stage 4: Style Feature Caching and KV Fusion for Content Images.} We first identify the target UNet block \texttt{up\_blocks.0.attentions.1}, then cache the style image K and V features from the selected self-attention processors within that block, and fuse them with content features in the corresponding processors during the reverse diffusion generation process of the content image. At each timestep: (1) during the U-Net forward pass of the style image's corresponding latent, record the K/V features produced by the selected self-attention processors in \texttt{up\_blocks.0.attentions.1}; (2) during the content latent denoising process, for the selected self-attention processors in this block, perform weighted fusion of the currently computed content K and V with the cached style K and V; (3) use the fused K/V for self-attention computation; (4) for non-target processors, keep the original K/V unchanged.

\medskip
\noindent{\bfseries Stage 5: Decoding and Output.} After reverse diffusion completes, obtain the latent representation of the generated image. Feed it into the VAE decoder to produce the final stylized image.

\subsection{Selective KV Fusion}

\subsubsection{Review of Self-Attention Mechanism}

In U-Net, the self-attention layer is computed as follows. Given features $X$ output by a residual block, three projection matrices first yield the query $Q$, key $K$, and value $V$:
\begin{equation}
Q = X W_Q, \quad K = X W_K, \quad V = X W_V \label{eq:qkv}
\end{equation}
The output is then computed via scaled dot-product attention:
\begin{equation}
\text{Attention}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}\!\left(\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V \label{eq:attention}
\end{equation}
where $d_k$ is the dimensionality of the query vectors. In standard generation, $Q$, $K$, and $V$ all originate from features of the same image. However, in style transfer tasks, we combine the content image's $K$, $V$ with the style image's $K$, $V$ through weighted fusion. It can be approximately understood that the content-side $Q$ helps maintain the spatial organization of the current latent, while the fused $K/V$ influence the texture and appearance patterns retrieved by attention.

\subsubsection{Weighted KV Fusion}

Inspired by Style Injection [16], we operate on the K/V within the selected self-attention processors of the target block \texttt{up\_blocks.0.attentions.1}. Unlike directly replacing content image features with style image features, we adopt a weighted fusion formulation:
\begin{equation}
K_{\text{fused}} = (1 - \gamma) K_{\text{content}} + \gamma K_{\text{style}} \label{eq:kfusion}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
V_{\text{fused}} = (1 - \gamma) V_{\text{content}} + \gamma V_{\text{style}} \label{eq:vfusion}
\end{equation}
where $\gamma \in [0, 1]$ is the style fusion intensity coefficient. This design offers the following advantages: (1) {\bfseries Adjustable}: $\gamma=0$ fully retains content K/V, $\gamma=1$ fully uses style K/V, with smooth transition at intermediate values; (2) {\bfseries Training-Free}: style injection is achieved solely through linear interpolation, requiring no gradient optimization; (3) {\bfseries Stability}: weighted fusion is smoother than direct replacement, reducing generation artifacts.

\subsubsection{Selection of Target Block and Attention Processors}

Following the findings of InstantStyle [17] regarding effective region selection, we first identify the UNet target blocks that are more sensitive to style transfer. All StyleKV experiments in this paper are fixed within the single target block \texttt{up\_blocks.0.attentions.1}, rather than replacing K/V across multiple blocks. Within this block, a subset of self-attention processors is selected to participate in StyleKV fusion. Three processor count configurations are tested:
\begin{itemize}
  \setlength{\itemsep}{0pt}
\item {\bfseries 1 processor}: only 1 \texttt{attn1.processor} is selected within \texttt{up\_blocks.0.attentions.1} for KV fusion
\item {\bfseries 5 processors} (default): 5 \texttt{attn1.processor}s are selected within \texttt{up\_blocks.0.attentions.1} for KV fusion
\item {\bfseries 10 processors}: 10 self-attention processors are selected within \texttt{up\_blocks.0.attentions.1} for KV fusion (covering more target positions within this block)
\end{itemize}
Experiments show that the configuration with 5 self-attention processors in \texttt{up\_blocks.0.attentions.1} achieves the best balance between style transfer quality and content preservation.

\subsection{Cross-Modal AdaIN for Text and Image Conditions}

In artistic style transfer, color tones and overall atmosphere constitute a significant portion of style information. However, we observe that style injection through KV fusion alone often struggles to accurately capture the global color statistics of a given style---while textures and local patterns are successfully transferred, semantic and color distribution inconsistencies between text and image conditions may still persist.

Drawing on the cross-modal AdaIN idea from StyleStudio [31], we adapt it to the StyleKV framework and combine it with selective K/V fusion and Canny structural constraints to improve color coordination and conditional consistency. Unlike methods that require training new adapters, we do not train additional adapter parameters; instead, during DDIM inversion and conditional injection, we apply AdaIN at the target UNet block for cross-modal fusion of the already-embedded text features and image features. The style image embedding in this paper is extracted by a pretrained image encoder; in the experimental implementation, we use the image encoder from the IP-Adapter configuration. AdaIN modulates the text conditional features with the channel statistics of this image embedding, aligning the text semantic guidance with the visual statistics of the reference style image.

Given text embedding features $f_{\text{text}}$ and style image embedding features $f_{\text{style}}$, cross-modal AdaIN is defined as:
\begin{equation}
f_{\text{adain}} = \sigma(f_{\text{style}}) \left( \frac{f_{\text{text}} - \mu(f_{\text{text}})}{\sigma(f_{\text{text}})} \right) + \mu(f_{\text{style}}) \label{eq:adain}
\end{equation}
where $\mu(\cdot)$ and $\sigma(\cdot)$ denote the mean and standard deviation along the channel dimension, respectively. In the code implementation, hyperparameters $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are further introduced, where $\alpha$ controls the linear mixing between the original text features and the AdaIN features, and $\beta$ scales the shift term in the AdaIN branch:
\begin{equation}
f_{\text{adain}}^{(\beta)} = \sigma(f_{\text{style}}) \left( \frac{f_{\text{text}} - \mu(f_{\text{text}})}{\sigma(f_{\text{text}})} \right) + \beta \mu(f_{\text{style}})
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
f_{\text{out}} = (1-\alpha) f_{\text{text}} + \alpha f_{\text{adain}}^{(\beta)}.
\end{equation}
Our experiments default to $\alpha=1.0$ and $\beta=1.0$, thus reducing to the standard AdaIN form shown in Eq.~\ref{eq:adain}. This operation preserves the semantic direction in the text embedding while aligning its channel statistics to the style image embedding, thereby enhancing the consistency between text and visual style conditions without relying on additional adapter training.

\subsection{ControlNet Integration}

To preserve the spatial structure of the content image, we integrate Canny ControlNet. ControlNet takes the Canny edge map as conditional input, and its output is added to the U-Net skip connections and middle block. Its design advantages include: (1) zero-convolution layers ensure training stability at initialization; (2) in the original ControlNet training paradigm, the original model parameters are frozen and only the ControlNet branch is trained; (3) plug-and-play without modifying the original model architecture. We directly use pretrained ControlNet weights matching the base model version, requiring no further training.

\subsection{Conditional Generation at Inference}

We do not retrain the diffusion model; instead, we inject Canny structural conditions, cross-modal AdaIN conditions, and selective KV fusion during inference of the pretrained Stable Diffusion. Given timestep $t$, text prompt $c$, Canny condition $c_{\text{canny}}$, and current latent $z_t$, the pretrained noise prediction network can be expressed as:
\begin{equation}
\hat{\epsilon} = \epsilon_\theta(z_t, t, c, c_{\text{canny}}) \label{eq:inference}
\end{equation}
where the parameters $\epsilon_\theta$ remain frozen. AdaIN StyleKV performs modulation only on the conditional features at the target UNet block, and on the K/V features of the selected self-attention processors in \texttt{up\_blocks.0.attentions.1} during inference, subsequently generating stylized images according to the five-stage pipeline described above.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

\subsubsection{Datasets}

{\bfseries Content dataset:} We use 5000 content images from the MS COCO 2017 dataset [18], each accompanied by a corresponding text prompt to assist the diffusion model in understanding the image content.

{\bfseries Style dataset:} We use 5000 style images from the WikiArt dataset [19], covering various artistic styles such as Impressionism, Cubism, and Abstract art.

{\bfseries Image pairing:} 5000 content images and 5000 style images are paired one-to-one in sequential order, yielding 5000 pairs in total.

{\bfseries Data scale:} A total of 5000 stylized images are generated for quantitative evaluation.

\subsubsection{Evaluation Metrics}

It should be noted that all three metrics in this paper are used in the ``lower is better'' direction, but they measure different aspects: Art-FID aligns with the style domain, FID aligns with the content domain, and LPIPS measures per-pair perceptual change. Since style transfer inherently involves a content--style trade-off, the three metrics typically cannot be simultaneously optimal and should be interpreted jointly.

{\bfseries Art-FID:} A comprehensive metric specifically designed for artistic image generation [20]. In our implementation, Art-FID measures the distribution distance between the set of generated images and the set of target style images, with lower values indicating closer proximity of generated results to the style domain. This is the primary evaluation metric in this paper.

{\bfseries FID (Fr\'{e}chet Inception Distance) [21]:} Measures the distribution distance between generated and content images in the InceptionV3 feature space. In this task, lower FID only indirectly reflects the overall distributional proximity of generated results to the content domain and should not be used alone as a structure preservation metric.

{\bfseries LPIPS (Learned Perceptual Image Patch Similarity) [22]:} Computes the perceptual similarity between stylized images and content images on a per-pair basis, based on pretrained deep network feature differences. Under the same task settings, lower LPIPS generally indicates smaller perceptual change relative to the content image. Since stylization inevitably introduces perceptual changes, we interpret LPIPS jointly with FID and Art-FID rather than using it alone as a style transfer quality metric.

\subsubsection{Device Resource Constraints}

Diffusion models (Stable Diffusion base model $\sim$7--8\,GB, plus VAE, ControlNet, and feature caching modules, totaling $\sim$15--20\,GB of VRAM) still pose certain challenges for consumer-grade GPUs. Even with a V100-32\,GB, batch generation faces VRAM pressure and GPU resource contention. Compared with performing injection across more blocks or processors, our approach adopts selective processor injection within \texttt{up\_blocks.0.attentions.1} (5 self-attention processors by default), thereby controlling the additional computation and caching overhead so that the entire pipeline can run smoothly on a V100.

\section{Interactive Frontend}

To enhance practical usability and user experience, we built an interactive frontend called \textbf{StyleKV Control Studio} based on the Gradio framework.

\subsection{Frontend Functional Modules}

{\bfseries (1) Image Input Module:} content image upload, style image upload, automatic generation and preview of the Canny edge map.

{\bfseries (2) Prompt Control Module:} positive prompt (describing the desired generation result), negative prompt (elements to avoid), with support for long text input.

{\bfseries (3) ControlNet/Canny Parameter Adjustment:} Canny Low/High Threshold (edge detection sensitivity), Conditioning Scale (ControlNet control strength), Bilateral Filter parameters (edge preprocessing denoising).

{\bfseries (4) AdaIN Style Fusion Module:} AdaIN Alpha (fusion intensity), AdaIN Beta (fusion bias), with real-time preview support.

{\bfseries (5) Generation Parameter Control:} Steps (DDIM sampling steps, default 50), CFG Scale (classifier-free guidance, default 7.5), Seed (random seed), KV fusion intensity $\gamma$.

{\bfseries (6) Output Management:} output directory setting, real-time generation status display, result gallery showcase.

\subsection{Frontend Features and Value}

The frontend supports hot parameter reloading (adjusting parameters without restarting to view results), real-time Canny edge map preview, saving configurations as JSON files, and batch generation. The design goal is to lower the usage barrier, enabling non-expert users to understand the impact of each parameter on generation results through intuitive sliders, switches, and preview images. \section{Future Work}

\subsection{Quantitative Comparison with Existing Methods}
Our current experiments focus on self-ablation to verify the effectiveness of each component and have not yet been compared with methods such as InST [12], StyleDiffusion [13], and InstantStyle [17] under equivalent conditions. We plan to conduct systematic inter-method comparisons under a unified dataset and evaluation protocol.

\subsection{Adaptive Canny Edge Map Generation}
Currently, Canny thresholds must be manually adjusted, and optimal values vary depending on image content. We plan to introduce better edge extraction models such as HED and RINDNet to realize adaptive edge map generation, reducing the need for manual parameter tuning.

\subsection{VLM-Based Automatic Prompt Generation}
Currently, prompts need to be manually written or generated with the help of external models. We plan to introduce open-source vision-language models (e.g., Qwen-VLM, LLaVA) to automatically generate high-quality prompts for style images, further lowering the usage barrier.

\subsection{Cross-Architecture Adaptation and Lightweighting}
Our method is validated on SDXL, and its effective attention processor selection and fusion strategy is architecture-dependent. We plan to adapt the KV fusion mechanism to other diffusion architectures such as SD1.5 and SD3, re-identify the effective self-attention processors under each architecture, and explore lightweighting schemes such as distillation and quantization to reduce dependence on high-VRAM devices.

\subsection{Incorporating Tile ControlNet}
High-resolution Canny edge maps may introduce artifacts. We plan to use Tile ControlNet to mitigate artifact issues at high resolution through patch-wise processing while maintaining detail richness.

\subsection{Processor-Level Contribution Analysis}
Currently, we only test naive AdaIN configurations with 1 or 2 target blocks, and StyleKV configurations with 1, 5, or 10 self-attention processors in \texttt{up\_blocks.0.attentions.1}. We plan to individually test the style transfer contribution of different self-attention processors in U-Net, establishing a processor-to-style mapping to provide a theoretical basis for method optimization.

\subsection{Multi-Condition ControlNet Fusion}
Currently, only Canny ControlNet is integrated. We plan to explore the synergistic operation of multiple ControlNets (edge + depth + pose, etc.) to further enhance content preservation capability.

\section{Conclusion and Limitations}

\subsection{Conclusion}

This paper proposes AdaIN StyleKV, a training-free style transfer method. The main contributions include:

\begin{itemize}
  \setlength{\itemsep}{0pt}
\item {\bfseries Methodological innovation:} We propose a weighted fusion strategy for style image KV and content image KV, achieving precise manipulation of self-attention layer features. Drawing on the cross-modal AdaIN idea from StyleStudio [31], we adapt it to our StyleKV framework and combine it with selective K/V fusion and Canny structural constraints to improve color coordination and conditional consistency.
\item {\bfseries Systematic validation:} We construct 7 method variants on the COCO+WikiArt dataset and generate 5000 images for component ablation, variant comparison, and parameter sensitivity analysis. Experiments demonstrate that the full method achieves a favorable balance between style fidelity and content preservation.
\item {\bfseries Practical value:} We build an interactive Gradio-based frontend supporting Canny adjustment, AdaIN parameter control, real-time comparison, and other features.
\item {\bfseries Empirical insights:} We document key experimental findings in detail, including the importance of prompt quality and the trade-off between Canny resolution and inference time.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Limitations}

Although our method achieves favorable results, the following limitations remain, as listed in Table~\ref{tab:limitations}. These limitations also constitute the key focus areas of the future work outlined in Section~6.

\subsection{Future Outlook}

Style transfer is a vibrant research field. With the continuous development of diffusion models, the following directions are worth sustained attention: (1) further optimization of training-free methods to achieve more precise style control while preserving content structure; (2) multi-modal style fusion, combining text descriptions, reference images, color palettes, and other conditions; (3) interpretability of style transfer---understanding the role of different U-Net layers in style transfer; (4) lightweight deployment---compressing and deploying the method on mobile devices.
