%% 1. Introduction
%% ================================================
\section{Introduction}

Artistic style transfer aims to combine the content of one image with the visual style of another. A useful result should preserve the scene structure of the content image while transferring texture, brushwork, and color from the reference style image.

Early work synthesized textures through non-parametric sampling [1]. Gatys et al. [2] later formulated style transfer as optimization over Gram matrices of VGG features. Feedforward networks [3,4] and Adaptive Instance Normalization (AdaIN) [4] made arbitrary style transfer much faster, while GAN-based methods improved image fidelity [5,6,7]. These methods still have limits. GAN-based models often generalize poorly outside the style domains seen during training, and optimization-based methods remain too slow for interactive use. Most early methods also provide little direct control over the balance between style strength and content preservation.

Diffusion models changed the design space for image generation [8,9,10]. Stable Diffusion [11] combines latent-space denoising with cross-attention conditioning, which makes high-resolution synthesis controllable through text prompts. Several diffusion-based style transfer methods have followed [12,13,14,15,16,17]. InST [12] and StyleDiffusion [13] can produce strong results, but they require per-image optimization or textual inversion. Training-free methods such as DiffStyle [14] and Style Injection [16] avoid this cost, but they may alter the content geometry or fail to capture global color statistics. Self-attention is useful for local texture transfer, yet it does not by itself enforce palette consistency. Continuous control over style strength is also uncommon.

AdaIN StyleKV addresses these issues with three inference-time mechanisms inside a frozen SDXL model. The project began with a simple AdaIN prototype using Canny ControlNet and the target-block selection suggested by InstantStyle [17]. Applying AdaIN to one or two UNet blocks improved color consistency, but local brushwork and texture transfer remained weak. We therefore added a Style Injection inspired K/V fusion step. Instead of replacing content K/V features with style K/V features, we blend them with a coefficient $\gamma \in [0,1]$. The fusion is restricted to selected self-attention processors in \texttt{up\_blocks.0.attentions.1}, rather than being applied across the whole UNet.

The AdaIN branch handles the color statistics that K/V fusion does not model explicitly. We adapt the cross-modal AdaIN formulation from StyleStudio [31] and apply it at the same target UNet block. A pretrained image encoder, IP-Adapter's image encoder in our implementation, extracts a style embedding. Its channel-wise mean and variance are then used to modulate text-condition features. Canny ControlNet supplies the spatial anchor through an edge map, while K/V fusion transfers local texture.

We evaluate seven variants on 5000 COCO [18] $\times$ WikiArt [19] pairs. Art-FID [20] measures proximity to the style domain, FID [21] measures distributional distance from the content domain, and LPIPS [22] measures per-pair perceptual change. The 5-processor StyleKV variant without AdaIN obtains the lowest Art-FID (85.43), but its LPIPS is 0.629. Adding AdaIN increases Art-FID to 90.76 while reducing LPIPS to 0.599, suggesting a more conservative but more stable perceptual change. We also analyze the effects of block count, processor count, $\gamma$, and AdaIN, and provide the AdaIN StyleKV project repository with a Gradio interface for interactive parameter control [32].

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Neural Style Transfer}

Gatys et al. [2] introduced neural style transfer by matching Gram matrices of VGG [23] features in an optimization loop. Johnson et al. [24] trained feedforward transformation networks with perceptual losses, and Huang \& Belongie [4] proposed AdaIN for arbitrary style transfer in a single forward pass. Later work explored attention mechanisms [25], Transformers [26], reversible flows [27], and dual-branch learning [28]. These methods improved speed and generalization, but highly abstract or non-photorealistic styles can still produce blurred details or flattened textures.

\subsection{GAN-Based Style Transfer}

CycleGAN [5] used cycle-consistency losses for unpaired image translation, and StarGAN [6] extended this setting to multiple domains in a single model. StyleGAN [7] and later versions [29,30] provide a controllable latent space for high-quality image synthesis. However, StyleGAN is not designed for cross-domain translation. Applying it to arbitrary style transfer usually requires additional fine-tuning or feature disentanglement, and the results can be unstable outside the training distribution.

\subsection{Diffusion-Based Style Transfer}

Diffusion models now lead in high-quality image generation. Among them, latent diffusion models (LDM) [11] achieve efficient training and inference by compressing images into a low-dimensional latent space. For style transfer, researchers have proposed several diffusion-based methods:

InST [12] uses textual inversion to embed a style image into the text token space, but each new style requires a separate inversion run. StyleDiffusion [13] combines CLIP-based losses with fine-tuning to separate content and style in feature space. DiffStyle [14] operates in the diffusion H-space without training, although direct application to Stable Diffusion may shift semantic layout. Style Injection [16] replaces self-attention K/V features with style-image features, giving a training-free route to texture transfer, but it has no explicit spatial anchor. InstantStyle [17] shows that only a subset of UNet blocks is strongly related to style injection. StyleStudio [31] studies selective control of style elements in text-driven style transfer.

\subsection{Conditional Control in Diffusion Models}

ControlNet [15] adds spatial conditions such as edges, depth, and pose to a frozen diffusion model through zero-convolution layers. We use the Canny variant. It takes an edge map from the content image, processes it through the ControlNet branch, and injects the resulting features into the frozen UNet. The ControlNet weights must match the base model. All experiments use SDXL with \texttt{diffusers/controlnet-canny-sdxl-1.0}. An SD1.5 setup would require a matched weight such as \texttt{lllyasviel/control\_v11p\_sd15\_canny}. We use SDXL for its higher native resolution and stronger semantic representation.

\section{Method}

AdaIN StyleKV uses a frozen SDXL backbone with a Canny ControlNet. At inference time, it combines cross-modal AdaIN for aligning text-condition features with style-image color statistics, selective KV fusion for injecting style K/V features into selected self-attention processors, and Canny edge guidance for preserving scene geometry. The pipeline has five stages.

\subsection{Overall Framework}

\medskip
\noindent{\bfseries Stage 1: Preprocessing.} Content and style images go into the VAE encoder, producing latents $z_{\text{content}}$ and $z_{\text{style}}$. A Canny edge detector (thresholds adjustable in our frontend) extracts a contour map from the content image as the ControlNet spatial condition $c_{\text{canny}}$.

\medskip
\noindent{\bfseries Stage 2: DDIM Inversion.} We run DDIM inversion on both latents separately to obtain noise trajectories $\{z_t^{\text{content}}\}$ and $\{z_t^{\text{style}}\}$ across all $T$ timesteps. These trajectories provide the timestep-aligned content and style latents used for AdaIN modulation and KV caching.

\medskip
\noindent{\bfseries Stage 3: Cross-Modal AdaIN.} At each denoising timestep, before the UNet forward pass, we modify the text-condition embeddings at the target block(s). AdaIN uses channel statistics from a pretrained style-image embedding, extracted with the IP-Adapter image encoder in our implementation. No additional adapter parameters are trained.

\medskip
\noindent{\bfseries Stage 4: KV Fusion.} The target block is \texttt{up\_blocks.0.attentions.1}. During the style image forward pass at timestep $t$, we record $\{K_{\text{style}}, V_{\text{style}}\}$ from selected self-attention processors in that block. During content denoising at the same timestep, each selected processor uses:
\[
\begin{aligned}
K_{\text{fused}} &= (1-\gamma)K_{\text{content}} + \gamma K_{\text{style}},\\
V_{\text{fused}} &= (1-\gamma)V_{\text{content}} + \gamma V_{\text{style}},
\end{aligned}
\quad \gamma \in [0,1].
\]
Non-target processors run standard self-attention with no caching and no blending. The content query $Q_{\text{content}}$ stays untouched throughout.

\medskip
\noindent{\bfseries Stage 5: Decoding.} After $T$ denoising steps, $z_0$ goes through the VAE decoder to produce the stylized output.

\subsection{Selective KV Fusion}

\subsubsection{Self-Attention Recap}

The UNet self-attention layer projects a feature map $X$ into query, key, and value:
\begin{equation}
Q = X W_Q, \quad K = X W_K, \quad V = X W_V \label{eq:qkv}
\end{equation}
then computes:
\begin{equation}
\text{Attention}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}\!\left(\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V \label{eq:attention}
\end{equation}
In standard generation, $Q$, $K$, and $V$ come from the same image. In our setting, $Q$ remains content-derived, while $K$ and $V$ are blended with style-derived features. This keeps the attention query tied to the content layout while allowing the retrieved features to carry texture and appearance from the style image.

  \label{fig:unet_arch}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Weighted KV Fusion}

Following Style Injection [16], we operate on self-attention processors inside \texttt{up\_blocks.0.attentions.1}. Unlike hard replacement, we use a linear blend:
\begin{equation}
K_{\text{fused}} = (1 - \gamma) K_{\text{content}} + \gamma K_{\text{style}} \label{eq:kfusion}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
V_{\text{fused}} = (1 - \gamma) V_{\text{content}} + \gamma V_{\text{style}} \label{eq:vfusion}
\end{equation}
where $\gamma \in [0, 1]$ controls style strength. When $\gamma=0$, the processor uses the content K/V. When $\gamma=1$, it uses the style K/V. Intermediate values interpolate between the two. This operation requires no gradient updates and is less abrupt than direct K/V replacement.

\subsubsection{Target Block and Processor Selection}

InstantStyle [17] shows that only some UNet blocks are strongly related to style injection. We therefore fix a single decoder block, \texttt{up\_blocks.0.attentions.1}, as the StyleKV injection site. All StyleKV experiments in this paper operate only in that block. We vary the number of participating self-attention processors:

\begin{itemize}
  \setlength{\itemsep}{0pt}
\item {\bfseries 1 proc}: minimal StyleKV injection
\item {\bfseries 5 procs}: default configuration
\item {\bfseries 10 procs}: stronger StyleKV injection with higher risk of detail loss
\end{itemize}

The 5-processor configuration gives the best balance between style transfer and content preservation in our experiments, so we use it as the default unless stated otherwise.

\subsection{Cross-Modal AdaIN for Text and Image Conditions}

KV fusion mainly affects local texture and brushwork. It does not reliably capture the global palette of the style image. Following the cross-modal AdaIN idea in StyleStudio [31], we use image-feature statistics to modulate text-condition features and improve color consistency.

A frozen image encoder, the IP-Adapter image encoder in our implementation, produces a style embedding $f_{\text{style}}$. At the same target UNet block used for KV fusion, we take the existing text-condition features $f_{\text{text}}$ and re-normalize them:
\begin{equation}
f_{\text{adain}} = \sigma(f_{\text{style}}) \left( \frac{f_{\text{text}} - \mu(f_{\text{text}})}{\sigma(f_{\text{text}})} \right) + \mu(f_{\text{style}}) \label{eq:adain}
\end{equation}
where $\mu(\cdot)$ and $\sigma(\cdot)$ are computed per channel. The implementation exposes two coefficients, $\alpha$ and $\beta$:
\begin{equation}
f_{\text{adain}}^{(\beta)} = \sigma(f_{\text{style}}) \left( \frac{f_{\text{text}} - \mu(f_{\text{text}})}{\sigma(f_{\text{text}})} \right) + \beta \mu(f_{\text{style}})
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
f_{\text{out}} = (1-\alpha) f_{\text{text}} + \alpha f_{\text{adain}}^{(\beta)}.
\end{equation}
With the default setting $\alpha{=}1.0$, $\beta{=}1.0$, this reduces to Eq.~\ref{eq:adain}. The operation keeps the semantic direction of the text embedding while matching its channel statistics to the style embedding. This changes the color and atmosphere implied by the condition without introducing new trainable modules.

\subsection{ControlNet Integration}

Canny ControlNet provides the spatial constraint. It receives the content image edge map and injects the resulting features into the frozen UNet through the ControlNet branch. We use the SDXL-matched weights \texttt{diffusers/controlnet-canny-sdxl-1.0} without further training.

\subsection{Inference}

Everything above happens at inference time inside a completely frozen $\epsilon_\theta$. At timestep $t$, with text prompt $c$ and Canny condition $c_{\text{canny}}$, the noise prediction is:
\begin{equation}
\hat{\epsilon} = \epsilon_\theta(z_t, t, c, c_{\text{canny}}) \label{eq:inference}
\end{equation}
The AdaIN modulation and KV fusion are injected as side-effects during the forward pass through the target block; the model weights never move. The output follows the five-stage pipeline described earlier.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

\subsubsection{Datasets}

{\bfseries Content dataset:} We use 5000 content images from the MS COCO 2017 dataset [18], each accompanied by a corresponding text prompt to assist the diffusion model in understanding the image content.

{\bfseries Style dataset:} We use 5000 style images from the WikiArt dataset [19], covering various artistic styles such as Impressionism, Cubism, and Abstract art.

{\bfseries Image pairing:} 5000 content images and 5000 style images are paired one-to-one in sequential order, yielding 5000 pairs in total.

{\bfseries Data scale:} A total of 5000 stylized images are generated for quantitative evaluation.

\subsubsection{Evaluation Metrics}

We track three numbers, all in the lower-is-better direction, but they capture different things. {\bfseries Art-FID} [20] measures distributional distance from the target style set; it is our primary gauge of how well the style came through. {\bfseries FID} [21] does the same against the content set, giving a rough proxy for content-domain proximity (though it's too global to serve as a standalone structure-preservation score). {\bfseries LPIPS} [22] computes per-pair perceptual distance between each output and its content image. A low LPIPS means the image ``reads'' similar to the original; a high LPIPS means the transfer visibly shifted things. Since any interesting stylization will push LPIPS up somewhat, we read it alongside Art-FID and FID, not in isolation. No single metric tells the full story; the three-way trade-off is the point.

Finer Canny edge maps improve generation quality because the model receives more structural information. Excessively fine edge maps can also make the model over-respond to local edges, producing outlining artifacts, fragmented textures, or unnatural local details. Inference time increases markedly with Canny resolution, so the method needs a practical balance between detail and cost. Fig.~\ref{fig:canny_res} shows the difference in Canny edge granularity across resolutions. The frontend therefore lets users adjust Canny edge-detection parameters.

\subsubsection{Device Resource Constraints}

Diffusion models still pose certain challenges for consumer-grade GPUs. In our setup, the Stable Diffusion base model uses about 7 to 8\,GB of VRAM; adding VAE, ControlNet, and feature caching brings the total to about 15 to 20\,GB. Even with a V100-32\,GB, batch generation faces VRAM pressure and GPU resource contention. Compared with injection across more blocks or processors, our approach uses selective processor injection within \texttt{up\_blocks.0.attentions.1} (5 self-attention processors by default), which controls the extra computation and caching overhead.

\subsection{Frontend Functional Modules}

{\bfseries (1) Image Input Module:} content image upload, style image upload, automatic generation and preview of the Canny edge map.

\section{Future Work}

\subsection{Quantitative Comparison with Existing Methods}
Our current experiments focus on self-ablation to verify the effectiveness of each component and have not yet been compared with methods such as InST [12], StyleDiffusion [13], and InstantStyle [17] under equivalent conditions. We plan to conduct systematic inter-method comparisons under a unified dataset and evaluation protocol.

\subsection{Adaptive Canny Edge Map Generation}
Currently, Canny thresholds must be manually adjusted, and optimal values vary depending on image content. We plan to introduce better edge extraction models such as HED and RINDNet to realize adaptive edge map generation, reducing the need for manual parameter tuning.

\subsection{VLM-Based Automatic Prompt Generation}
Currently, prompts need to be manually written or generated with the help of external models. We plan to introduce open-source vision-language models (e.g., Qwen-VLM, LLaVA) to automatically generate high-quality prompts for style images, further lowering the usage barrier.

\subsection{Cross-Architecture Adaptation and Lightweighting}
Our method is validated on SDXL, and its effective attention processor selection and fusion strategy is architecture-dependent. We plan to adapt the KV fusion mechanism to other diffusion architectures such as SD1.5 and SD3, re-identify the effective self-attention processors under each architecture, and explore lightweighting schemes such as distillation and quantization to reduce dependence on high-VRAM devices.

\subsection{Incorporating Tile ControlNet}
High-resolution Canny edge maps may introduce artifacts. We plan to use Tile ControlNet to mitigate artifact issues at high resolution through patch-wise processing while maintaining detail richness.

\subsection{Processor-Level Contribution Analysis}
Currently, we only test naive AdaIN configurations with 1 or 2 target blocks, and StyleKV configurations with 1, 5, or 10 self-attention processors in \texttt{up\_blocks.0.attentions.1}. We plan to individually test the style transfer contribution of different self-attention processors in U-Net, establishing a processor-to-style mapping to provide a theoretical basis for method optimization.

\subsection{Multi-Condition ControlNet Fusion}
The current system only integrates Canny ControlNet. Future work will test multiple ControlNets, such as edge, depth, and pose, for stronger content preservation.

\FloatBarrier
\section{Conclusion and Limitations}

\subsection{Conclusion}

We put forward AdaIN StyleKV, a training-free style transfer pipeline that combines three lightweight mechanisms inside a frozen SDXL+ControlNet backbone: (1) selective KV fusion, which caches style K/V from a handful of self-attention processors in \texttt{up\_blocks.0.attentions.1} and blends them with content K/V via a single $\gamma$; (2) cross-modal AdaIN, adapted from StyleStudio [31], to pull text-condition features toward the style image's color statistics; and (3) Canny ControlNet for spatial anchoring. On 5000 COCO$\times$WikiArt pairs, the full configuration lands at Art-FID 90.76 with LPIPS 0.599, while the AdaIN-free StyleKV variant pushes Art-FID down to 85.43 at the cost of higher perceptual disruption (LPIPS 0.629). We also release a Gradio frontend that exposes every parameter to the user in real time.

\subsection{Limitations}

Although our method produces competitive results, the following limitations remain, as listed in Table~\ref{tab:limitations}. These limitations also constitute the key focus areas of the future work outlined in Section~6.

\begin{table}[htbp]
  \centering
  \caption{Major limitations of our method and corresponding improvement directions.}
  \label{tab:limitations}
  \small
  \setlength{\tabcolsep}{3pt}
  \begin{tabular}{>{\raggedright\arraybackslash}p{2.0cm}>{\raggedright\arraybackslash}p{3.0cm}>{\raggedright\arraybackslash}p{2.6cm}}
    \toprule
    Limitation & Description & Improvement \\
    \midrule
    No external SOTA comparison & Only self-variant ablation; not compared with InST, StyleDiffusion, InstantStyle, etc. & Reproduce and compare under unified protocol \\

    Single ControlNet & Only Canny tested; other modalities not explored & Multi-condition ControlNet fusion \\
    External prompt dependency & Prompts from external models; no integrated VLM & Integrate Qwen-VLM / LLaVA \\
    Limited evaluation metrics & FID, Art-FID, LPIPS only; no perceptual/semantic coverage & Add metrics + human subjective study \\
    High device requirements & Needs V100-class GPU; not consumer-friendly & Distillation \& quantization \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

\subsection{Future Outlook}

Style transfer is far from solved. As diffusion models keep evolving, a few directions strike us as especially worth pursuing: pushing training-free methods toward finer style disentanglement (so users can independently control brush scale, palette, and texture density); fusing multiple conditions (text, reference image, color palette) without them fighting each other; and mapping out which UNet layers actually matter for style, so future methods can be leaner by design. Getting these pipelines to run on consumer hardware, through distillation or quantization, would also go a long way toward making them useful outside the lab.
